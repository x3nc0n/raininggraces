# Raining Graces Blog — Style & Tone Guide

## Voice

Robin writes in a **warm, confident, faith-rooted first-person voice**. The tone
is conversational yet professional — like a knowledgeable friend sharing wisdom
over coffee. She speaks directly to expectant parents ("you," "your family") and
frequently weaves in her personal story (former police officer → stay-at-home
mom → doula) to build trust and relatability.

## Tone Keywords

- Compassionate & nurturing
- Calm & grounded
- Empowering (never fear-based)
- Faith-centered without being preachy
- Evidence-informed but accessible

## Structure & Formatting

- **Horizontal rules (`---`)** separate major sections — every post uses them
  liberally to create visual breathing room.
- **H2 headings** (`##`) introduce each section; they are descriptive and
  inviting, not clickbait-y.
- **Bulleted lists with bold lead-ins** are the signature move — nearly every
  section contains a list where each item starts with a bolded phrase followed
  by a softer explanation.
- **Emoji section markers** (🌀 🌿 🔍 🤰 💜 📣) are used in the Spinning
  Babies / educational posts to add warmth and scannability, but not in the
  personal-story posts.
- Posts are **800–1,200 words**, thorough but not exhaustive.
- A **closing paragraph** always ties back to Robin's mission — supporting
  families, honoring birth, and inviting connection.

## Content Pillars

1. **Personal story & calling** — Robin's journey from law enforcement to doula
   work, motherhood, faith.
2. **Education & evidence** — Spinning Babies® techniques, fetal positioning,
   comfort measures, what the research says.
3. **Empowerment & encouragement** — Helping parents feel confident, informed,
   and supported; normalizing questions and emotions.
4. **Community & local connection** — OKC-area focus, first-responder families,
   Catholic/faith families, local hospital landscape.
5. **Book & resource recommendations** — Curated reading lists with personal
   commentary on why each resource matters.

## Language Patterns

- Prefers em-dashes (—) for asides.
- Uses "Mama" (capitalized) when addressing the birthing parent warmly.
- Refers to God's guidance subtly ("When God nudged me…") rather than with
  heavy theological language.
- Avoids medical jargon unless immediately explained in plain terms.
- Frames challenges positively: "longer labor" becomes an opportunity for
  techniques and support, not a scare tactic.
